    Taxis in Israel. What I wish I had known.
    3 years ago

    Israel travel tip on Taxis: This was my first trip to Israel and I wish someone had told me a few things about this before I went. Most taxis do NOT take credit cards. Some say yes, when you ask them, but then say "no" when it's time to pay.

    I think we used taxis about a dozen times and I think only twice did they have the meter running. The rest of the time it was a flat rate charge. When departing the hotel it was easier to get a taxi that took credit cards and agreed to a pre-negotiated fee.

    Have plenty of Isaeli currency or US dollars to pay. They didn't seem to mind taking US dollars and we showed them on a smart phone the currency conversion and they were OK with that.

    We felt that for most of the taxi rides we took, we were taken advantage of to some extent. One tried to increase the cost by about 30% over what we had agreed to. If you like to negotiate prices you'll love this.

    Have precise full names and waze or google maps directions of where you are going to. We arrived at Tel Aviv airport and needed a ride to the Cramim resort. We probably talked to 6 drivers until we could find one that was able to figure out where we were going. Information and directions in Hebrew would be adviseable. This is especially true if your destination is not in Tel Aviv or Jerusalem.

    If you need a Taxi on Shabbat only a small percentage of taxis are operating. You will pay a LOT more and may need a connection.

    There's a webservice for Taxis but we never got to any level of proficiency. Several times it took us a half an hour to flag down a Taxi.

    We didn't realize that our trip coincided with all the religious holidays. Lots of people traveling around. Taxis were harder than usual to find. We had a number of times where they turned us down because where we were gong to was out of their way and didn't offer the likelihood of a return fare.

    Next time I'll likely try to rent a car instead of relying on taxis. Just need to factor the hotel parking costs and different aggravation factors..

    5. Re: Taxis in Israel. What I wish I had known.

    Welcome to the Israel forum.

    It is a shame that you didn't come here first. We would have guided you on how to use taxis. I am going to write out some general guidelines. While it is too late for you--due to the title of your thread, it may help others. However, I am curious--what "all the religious holidays" were you in Israel for? Unless you were there pre-pandemic, Israel has not been open to tourists for any major holiday ( Passover starts tomorrow eve--I'm taking a break from preparations)

    A. Taxi at airport. --there is an official taxi line. The dispatcher will get you a taxi. The taxis at the airport DO go all over the country.

    B. Meters--Taxis within a city are supposed to use the meter. When you get it you tell them you want the meter AND a recipe. You can watch the meter as you go. There are extra charges for suitcases. If you are traveling without suitcases the meter should not jump at the end. If so, ask why ( I once did, and like magic--it jumped back down) Just having the driver KNOW you want the receipt helps since the receipt is itemized, includes cab number and driver number. It CAN be used to report a driver and while you may not do that--they do not know that! They also do not know who you are sharing it with. i.e. you Israeli cousin who is fluent!

    C. Generally, unless you are paying through an app there will NOT be credit card acceptance. Especially with the increase in gas prices, the drivers, understandably do not want to have to pay the charges.

    D. Like in NYC, get into the taxi and tell them where you are going.

    E. Many cabs, in addition to driving looking for people to flag them down also have dispatch companies. Ask here, ask the hotel etc. for numbers.

    F. Download the GETT app. It DOES work.

    8. Re: Taxis in Israel. What I wish I had known.
    3 years ago

    I write to respectfully disagree with and I hope to clarify what Paul has written. Not a dig at Paul at all -- but in 18 trips, I have learned a lot about using taxis.

    • Never plan on being able to use credit cards, and it's a terrible idea to us US dollars. You will always pay. And who would expect to be able to use euros when taking cabs in the US? The currency is shekels, easily obtained using your US ATM card.

    • Paul did not mention this, but it is not the custom to tip cab drivers unless they do something above-and-beyond, like carrying your luggage into the hotel.

    • Do NOT accept a "pre-negotiated fee" for travel within a city unless you are VERY familiar with what the fare will be. You will always be overcharged if you do that. Israeli cab drivers are REQUIRED BY LAW to use the meter for travel within a cities, and if the driver will not turn on the meter or lies and says that he does not have one, get out. It's your absolute right. I've done it many times. An easy way is to say at the outset that you will need a receipt, then they will have to use the meter. (Or use the GETT app, as mentioned above.) But park the "American nice" and insist on the meter.

    • The only time that a pre-set fee is appropriate is trips between cities, and then the rates are fixed by regulations. Ask at your hotel or do an internet search to find out that fee.

    I don't want to make it out that all taxi drivers will cheat you -- most will not -- but yes, Paul, you did make some first-time-visitor mistakes that we routinely caution against on this forum. We appreciate you sharing your experience, but your post still contains incorrect advice (negotiating the fixed price, for example), so I wrote this to clarify for others who might read this thread.

    Boiled down: Use the meter or use GETT always except when going between cities.

    Douglas Duckett
